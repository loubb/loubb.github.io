  9 # If— Rudyard Kipling - 1865-1936
 10 
 11 If you can keep your head when all about you
 12    Are losing theirs and blaming it on you;
 13 If you can trust yourself when all men doubt you,
 14    But make allowance for their doubting too;
 15 If you can wait and not be tired by waiting,
 16    Or, being lied about, don’t deal in lies,
 17 Or, being hated, don’t give way to hating,
 18    And yet don’t look too good, nor talk too wise;
 19 
 20 If you can dream—and not make dreams your master;
 21    If you can think—and not make thoughts your aim;
 22 If you can meet with triumph and disaster
 23    And treat those two impostors just the same;
 24 If you can bear to hear the truth you’ve spoken
 25    Twisted by knaves to make a trap for fools,
 26 Or watch the things you gave your life to broken,
 27    And stoop and build ’em up with wornout tools;
 28 
 29 If you can make one heap of all your winnings
 30    And risk it on one turn of pitch-and-toss,
 31 And lose, and start again at your beginnings
 32    And never breathe a word about your loss;
 33 If you can force your heart and nerve and sinew
 34    To serve your turn long after they are gone,
 35 And so hold on when there is nothing in you
 36    Except the Will which says to them: “Hold on”;
 37 
 38 If you can talk with crowds and keep your virtue,
 39    Or walk with kings—nor lose the common touch;
 40 If neither foes nor loving friends can hurt you;
 41    If all men count with you, but none too much;
 42 If you can fill the unforgiving minute
 43 With sixty seconds’ worth of distance run—
 44    Yours is the Earth and everything that’s in it,
 45 And—which is more—you’ll be a Man, my son!

 49 # Hamlet (by Shakespeare) - To Be or Not To Be
 50 
 51 To be, or not to be, that is the question:
 52 Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer
 53 The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune,
 54 Or to take Arms against a Sea of troubles,
 55 And by opposing end them: to die, to sleep
 56 No more; and by a sleep, to say we end
 57 The heart-ache, and the thousand natural shocks
 58 That Flesh is heir to? 'Tis a consummation
 59 Devoutly to be wished. To die, to sleep,
 60 To sleep, perchance to Dream; aye, there's the rub,
 61 For in that sleep of death, what dreams may come,
 62 When we have shuffled off this mortal coil,
 63 Must give us pause. There's the respect
 64 That makes Calamity of so long life:
 65 For who would bear the Whips and Scorns of time,
 66 The Oppressor's wrong, the proud man's Contumely, [F: poore]
 67 The pangs of dispised Love, the Law’s delay, [F: dispriz’d]
 68 The insolence of Office, and the spurns
 69 That patient merit of th'unworthy takes,
 70 When he himself might his Quietus make
 71 With a bare Bodkin? Who would Fardels bear, [F: these Fardels]
 72 To grunt and sweat under a weary life,
 73 But that the dread of something after death,
 74 The undiscovered country, from whose bourn
 75 No traveller returns, puzzles the will,
 76 And makes us rather bear those ills we have,
 77 Than fly to others that we know not of?
 78 Thus conscience does make cowards of us all,
 79 And thus the native hue of Resolution
 80 Is sicklied o'er, with the pale cast of Thought,
 81 And enterprises of great pitch and moment, [F: pith]
 82 With this regard their Currents turn awry, [F: away]
 83 And lose the name of Action. Soft you now,
 84 The fair Ophelia? Nymph, in thy Orisons
 85 Be all my sins remember'd.

 89 # Winston Churchill: House of Commons - 4 June 1940
 90 
 91 "The British Empire and the French Republic, linked together in their cause and in their need, will defend to the death their native soil, aiding each other like good comrades to the utmost of their strength.
 92 
 93 "Even though large tracts of Europe and many old and famous states have fallen or may fall into the grip of the Gestapo and all the odious apparatus of Nazi rule, we shall not flag or fail.
 94 
 95 "We shall go on to the end, we shall fight in France, we shall fight on the seas and oceans, we shall fight with growing confidence and growing strength in the air, we shall defend our island, whatever the cost may be.
 96 
 97 "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets, we shall fight in the hills; we shall never surrender, and even if, which I do not for a moment believe, this island or a large part of it were subjugated and starving, then our Empire beyond the seas, armed and guarded by the British fleet, would carry on the struggle, until, in God's good time, the new world, with all its power and might, steps forth to the rescue and the liberation of the old."
